My name is Enea Manzi, and I am writing to express my strong motivation to join the "Intersectoral Innovation" industrial PhD program, with reference to Research Theme 2, "Trusted Agentic Generative AI for Intent-Driven Deployment, Observability and Troubleshooting in Telco Edge-Cloud Continuum Systems", developed in collaboration with TIM S.p.A.

I am completing a Master's Degree in Cyber Security at the University of Milan, finalizing my thesis at the SESAR Lab (Secure Service-oriented Architectures Research Lab) on an automated security assurance tool for REST APIs, designed to be application-agnostic and contract-driven. Working on it, I had to confront a tension that recurs throughout the security literature: tools specialized on a single target go deep but lose portability, while tools generic enough to work everywhere often remain blind to semantics, reacting to the syntax of a request rather than to the contract that defines what it is actually allowed to do, and therefore lose depth. I addressed this by deriving all knowledge about the target at runtime, from its OpenAPI specification and a configuration file, so that the tool does not have to trade depth for portability. A closely related challenge is what the literature calls the oracle problem: generating requests against a system is well understood, but establishing automatically whether an observed behaviour constitutes a violation is not, since that judgement depends on domain knowledge no specification can provide on its own. Much of my work has gone into constructing systematic, declarative criteria, specified oracles grounded in domain knowledge rather than improvised heuristics, so the tool reaches a verifiable verdict instead of merely flagging anomalies.

A further challenge concerned the orchestration of the assessment itself, not just its individual checks. Tests can depend on one another, and executing them in the wrong order silently produces results that depend on scheduling rather than on the actual behaviour of the target. I addressed this with a dependency graph validated before any execution starts, so that cycles or unresolved prerequisites are caught as explicit, diagnosable errors rather than silent inconsistencies, and with an execution model where a failure in one check is isolated and reported rather than crashing the whole assessment. The result is a pipeline whose behaviour is deterministic and traceable end to end, a property that proved necessary for integrating the tool into continuous development cycles.

I see these experiences as relevant to Research Theme 2, though at a much smaller and more constrained scale than what the research line addresses. The research line aims at agentic Generative AI systems that interpret high-level intents and translate them into deployment and orchestration actions across the Telco edge-cloud continuum, remaining reliable, explainable and auditable rather than acting as an opaque decision layer; it also explicitly asks what architectural principles enable trusted multi-agent orchestration for planning, validation and execution. REST APIs are static, well-specified interfaces, while autonomous Telco systems operate under far greater uncertainty, so I do not want to overstate the parallel. What I do recognize, from having worked on a narrower version of it, is the underlying shape of the problem: keeping a system general enough to act across heterogeneous targets while staying grounded in their actual semantics, verifying decisions against an explicit criterion rather than trusting them blindly, and structuring execution so that failures are caught and explained rather than silently propagated. I also bring more modest, direct experience with the underlying infrastructure: during my Bachelor's thesis, also at SESAR Lab, I contributed to an assurance engine that collected telemetry from distributed systems and integrated Elasticsearch as a monitoring backend to evaluate their behaviour against declared non-functional contracts, deployed on Kubernetes; this gave me a first, concrete sense of what it means to decide what to observe and how to evaluate it continuously, rather than treating observability as an abstract requirement.

With reference to the same research theme, I expect this PhD to let me acquire competences that belong to a genuinely different scale of problem. Intents in this setting are expressed in natural language rather than a fixed specification, the action space is far larger, and the consequences of an unverified decision are operationally significant. I want to learn how to formalize trust and verification criteria for such less deterministic systems, how to design multi-agent architectures accountable to explicit policies, and how to evaluate trustworthiness over time rather than at a single point of assessment. Working alongside TIM S.p.A. would also let me develop a different methodological rigour: formulating and bounding an open-ended problem under real operational constraints, rather than the closed statements a Master's thesis affords.

My decision to pursue a PhD has a more personal root. What drives me, throughout my studies, has always been curiosity for what I do not yet know, and a strong preference for a specific way of learning it: watching how something is actually done, rather than reading about it in the abstract. When I do not know how to do something, my first instinct is to look for someone who does, and to learn by observing their approach to the problem. A PhD is, to me, the structure that most naturally supports this way of learning over an extended period, rather than in the fragments a job tends to allow. What specifically draws me to this program is the connection between research and development: working with genuinely new ideas, such as generative AI as a cognitive control layer for autonomous infrastructure, while keeping that work anchored to a solid technical foundation rather than a purely theoretical exercise. That combination of frontier ideas and grounded practice is what a three-year industrial PhD, with an academic and an industrial supervisor, is structurally built to provide.

A further element for the committee: part of my thesis work, developed with my research group at SESAR Lab, is being prepared as a publication for the ITADATA 2026 conference, a further sign that I can carry research through to a contribution the scientific community can evaluate, not only to a working implementation. More broadly, this thesis continues a line of inquiry pursued within the same group across both my Bachelor's and Master's degrees, on assurance and monitoring of distributed systems; I see this PhD, and Research Theme 2, as a natural continuation of that trajectory, extended to a domain where the questions are at once more difficult and more consequential.

I thank the committee for considering my application.
